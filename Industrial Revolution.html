The Industrial Revolution

The Industrial Revolution created changes in life expectancy and levels of wealth over approximately 200 years.
While the 'first' Industrial Revolution began in Britain in the mid 18th century, a 'second' Industrial Revolution occurred in Europe and other parts of the world in the nineteenth century.
This transition included going from hand production methods to machines, new chemical manufacturing and iron production processes, the increasing use of steam power and water power, the development of machine tools and the rise of the mechanized factory system.
Goods that had once been painstakingly crafted by hand started to be produced in mass quantities by machines in factories, thanks to the introduction of new machines and techniques in textiles, iron making and other industries.

Child Labour

In the early 1800's when the growth of factories started to rapidly accelerate, business owners relied heavily on cheap labour in order to keep up with demand.
To maximise profit, owners would pay women and children to work for much less than their male counterparts.
Long hours, low wages, working conditions and poverty led to many fatal accidents, and lowered life expectancy for people living in urban centres.
Child labour became a large problem but it wasn't addressed until 1837 in the House of Commons.
Many laws were passed that forced business owners to improve quality of working conditions.
The children were small enough to fit between machinery and their families were grateful for extra income. Education wasn't as common as it was not compulsory.
